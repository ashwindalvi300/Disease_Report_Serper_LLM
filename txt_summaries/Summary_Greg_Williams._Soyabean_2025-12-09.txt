

**===== CROP TYPE : SOYABEAN =====**

**===== DISEASE : BACTERIAL BLIGHT =====**

**Common Causes**
- Infection by *Pseudomonas syringae* pv. *glycinea* (or *Pseudomonas savastanoi* pv. *glycinea*), carried on crop residue, seed or splashed by rain/wind; entry through stomata or wounds; requires wet leaf surface; favored by cool, moist conditions (70–80 °F, 21–27 °C)
- Wet, windy conditions during early or mid‑season; over‑wintering inoculum in residue; seed transmission; cultivation when foliage is wet

**Organic Treatments**
- Copper sulfate or copper‑based bactericides applied early (within first 5–7 days of symptom appearance); sea‑weed or neem extracts for foliar application; application of compost tea or beneficial bacteria to suppress pathogen proliferation
- Adopting integrated cultural practices (crop rotation with non‑host crops, tillage of residue, use of resistant varieties) reduces inoculum

**Chemical Control Methods**
- Copper‑based bactericides (e.g., copper hydroxide) labeled for bacterial blight; must be applied early in the disease cycle to be effective
- Azoxystrobin and other fungicides are not effective against bacterial blight; avoid using them for this disease

**Future Prevention**
- Plant cultivars with known resistance or tolerance to bacterial blight; rotate soybeans with non‑host crops for at least one year; incorporate residue via tillage when feasible; avoid wet cultivation and keep foliage dry during rain events
- Use pathogen‑free seed and perform seed treatments with copper or other bactericides

**Remedies**
- Apply copper bactericide immediately if possible; monitor upper canopy for angular lesions with yellow halos; remove and destroy severely infected leaves and residues in the field; ensure adequate airflow to reduce leaf wetness; consider foliar nutrient support (e.g., nitrogen side‑dress) to improve plant vigor

**Additional Insights**
- Bacterial blight generally causes minor yield loss but can lead to leaf tattering, reduced photosynthesis, and seed quality issues; it often occurs early in the season but can persist until dry, hot conditions stop progress; it can be confused with Septoria brown spot—look for green lower canopy and yellow halo around lesions
- The field data shows moderate severity (0.7) at harvest with no recent rainfall; this indicates that early-season conditions favored the pathogen, and the disease has persisted despite dry current conditions; consultant recommendation of Azoxystrobin is not suitable for bacterial blight and should be revised to a copper-based treatment

**===== DISEASE : FROGEYE =====**

**Common Causes**
- Fungal pathogen Cercospora sojina (formerly Cerospora sojina) infects leaves, stems, pods, and seeds.
- Inoculum originates from infected seed, crop residue, or airborne spores.
- Infection peaks after flowering and during warm (25‑30 °C), humid conditions with high relative humidity (>90 %) and rainfall or splash.
- Continuous soybean cropping and reduced tillage increase inoculum load.
- Susceptible germplasm (especially non‑resistant varieties) exacerbates disease severity.

**Organic Treatments**
- Apply copper sulfate or copper hydroxide formulations as foliar sprays (2–4 % Cu) to reduce lesion development.
- Use sulfur dust or spray (2 % elemental sulfur) to inhibit spore germination and early infection.
- Neem oil (1–2 %) provides mild systemic protection and disrupts fungal growth.
- Incorporate well‑decomposed organic matter or cover crops to suppress residual inoculum.
- Ensure seed is certified disease‑free and treat with organic seed‑borne fungicides (e.g., chlorothalonil or potassium salts) if available.

**Chemical Control Methods**
- Azoxystrobin (strobilurin) – effective against C. sojina when applied in late flowering to early pod set; rotate with other classes (e.g., dimoxystrobin, iprodione, or triazoles) to avoid resistance.
- Trifloxystrobin or azoxystrobin + fludioxonil combinations offer broad control and lower resistance risk.
- Iprodione or chlorothalonil can be used as spot‑treatments on highly susceptible varieties when early lesions appear.
- Timing: apply at R3–R5 stages; a second application may be warranted if conditions remain favorable (warm, humid, rainfall).
- Follow label rates and safety guidelines; integrate with insect and nutrient management practices.

**Future Prevention**
- Plant certified resistant soybean varieties (Rcs genes such as Rcs3) to reduce initial inoculum and lesion development.
- Implement a 2‑year crop rotation with non‑host crops (corn, wheat, sorghum) to break the disease cycle.
- Employ deep or tillage practices to bury residue, minimizing overwintering inoculum.
- Use pathogen‑free seed and perform seed testing in storage facilities.
- Monitor weather forecasts; avoid late‑season heavy rainfall on fields with high disease pressure.
- Adopt integrated disease management, combining cultural, biological, and chemical controls.

**Remedies**
- Immediate foliar application of Azoxystrobin or an alternative effective fungicide if the field is at high risk (severity 0.9).
- For late‑season, consider a protective spray if rainfall or high humidity is expected to sustain lesion development.
- Perform post‑harvest residue management: shred or bury heavily infected straw to reduce inoculum for next season.
- Apply nitrogen side‑dress as recommended by the consultant to support plant vigor, which may help mitigate disease impact.

**Additional Insights**
- Field conditions (25.5 °C, 55 % humidity, no rainfall) are not ideal for rapid sporulation, but the high severity indicates a pre‑existing infection cycle that may persist.
- Because Frogeye is polycyclic, early control is critical; a single fungicide application later in the season may not fully prevent yield loss.
- Azoxystrobin is a strobilurin; resistance has been reported in some regions, so confirm local resistance monitoring before sole reliance on this class.
- The field’s cracked soil may limit uniform irrigation penetration; ensure adequate moisture distribution to support healthy canopy growth.
- Integrating the suggested practices can reduce disease pressure not only for Frogeye but also for co‑occurring Bacterial Blight, improving overall field resilience.
