

**===== CROP TYPE : CORN =====**

**===== DISEASE : GRAY LEAF SPOT =====**

**Common Causes**
- Caused by the fungus Cercospora zeae-maydis
- Favored by warm temperatures (77-86°F or 25-30°C) and high humidity (>90% for 12+ hours)
- Spread by wind and splashing water
- Survives in corn residue, more severe in corn following corn
- Infection typically occurs in June, with rapid spread in late July and August

**Organic Treatments**
- Crop rotation with non-corn crops to reduce fungus survival
- Conventional tillage to reduce survival of the fungus
- Removing corn residue to minimize inoculum
- Planting resistant corn hybrids

**Chemical Control Methods**
- Foliar fungicides labeled for gray leaf spot
- Fungicide applications between VT (tasseling) and R2 stages if economically warranted
- Scouting and early application of fungicides in high-risk fields

**Future Prevention**
- Use of resistant corn hybrids
- Crop rotation to reduce disease pressure
- Tillage to promote decomposition of corn debris
- Scouting fields regularly, especially during high-risk periods
- Reducing irrigation to lower canopy moisture

**Remedies**
- Early detection and management to prevent severe yield loss
- Monitoring environmental conditions favorable for disease development
- Consulting with agricultural experts for tailored management strategies

**Additional Insights**
- Gray leaf spot can be confused with other foliar diseases such as northern corn leaf blight and southern rust
- High humidity and warm conditions are critical for disease development
- The disease reduces photosynthetic surface area, leading to yield loss and potential stalk lodging
- Fields with a history of gray leaf spot should be managed with increased vigilance

**===== DISEASE : SOUTHERN RUST =====**

**Common Causes**
- Caused by the fungus Puccinia polysora.
- Spread by wind-blown spores from tropical regions.
- Favorable conditions include high humidity (80% or more), temperatures between 77-88°F (25-31°C), and at least 6 hours of leaf wetness.
- Young leaves are more susceptible to infection.

**Organic Treatments**
- Planting resistant corn varieties, especially for late-planted corn.
- Scouting fields early and frequently to monitor disease progression.
- Reducing irrigation to lower canopy moisture and leaf wetness.

**Chemical Control Methods**
- Applying fungicides between the silking (R1) and milk (R3) stages for maximum effectiveness.
- Using fungicides with multiple modes of action for better control.
- Considering a second fungicide application if disease pressure is severe and the crop is still in the reproductive stages.

**Future Prevention**
- Selecting corn products with genetic resistance to southern rust.
- Monitoring weather conditions and disease maps to predict and prepare for southern rust outbreaks.
- Implementing early and frequent scouting to detect and manage the disease before it spreads.

**Remedies**
- Applying fungicides to protect uninfected leaf tissue and reduce the spread of the disease.
- Reducing irrigation to minimize leaf wetness and slow disease development.
- Removing infected plant material to prevent further spread.

**Additional Insights**
- Southern rust can reduce photosynthesis, leading to premature plant drying, reduced kernel weight, and lowered yield potential.
- Yield losses as high as 45 percent have been reported with severe infections.
- Southern rust does not overwinter in most U.S. locations and must recolonize every year via wind-blown spores.
- The disease is more problematic in the southern United States due to more favorable conditions.
